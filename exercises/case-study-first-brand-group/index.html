Introduction & Overview

Could — or should — the books have flagged the risks?

The Case Background

In late September, First Brands Group (FBG), a privately held US auto parts company, filed for bankruptcy protection. The petition listed total liabilities of $10-50bn. FBG and related entities carried more than $8bn of secured corporate borrowings and inventory-backed financing. The central issue was its use of multiple forms of working capital financing, including leasing structures that were not clearly disclosed to other lenders.

Hidden Debt & Factoring

Rating agencies noted an increase in factoring, through which a company sells its unpaid invoices to a third party. Because factoring is usually structured as a sale rather than a loan, this did not add debt to FBG’s balance sheet, even though rating agencies treated them as debt-like when assessing leverage.

Inventory-backed funding was also used. Utah-based leasing firm Onset Financial, which had originally loaned equipment to FBG, became its largest creditor with a $1.9bn claim.

The Private Credit Boom & A Shelved $6bn Deal

The scale of this hidden leverage was fueled by FBG’s owner, Patrick James, whose strategy relied heavily on aggressive, fully debt-financed acquisitions. To fund this rapid expansion, FBG turned to the burgeoning private credit market. Yield-hungry alternative lenders—including Jefferies, UBS O’Connor, Pemberton, and Katsumi Global—stepped in to fill the gap left by traditional banks, providing opaque invoice financing and factoring lines that often yielded more than 10% (an unusually high rate given that FBG's customers were often investment-grade automotive retailers).

The situation reached a breaking point when FBG attempted to market a massive $6bn loan deal. Prospective investors began to balk at the opaque financial disclosures and the dearth of public information surrounding James. Their hesitation was compounded by the discovery that James had been sued a decade prior by Fortress Investment Group and Tristate Capital Bank over alleged fraud and misrepresentations of accounts receivable and inventory (both cases were ultimately settled and dismissed).

The Catalyst: Unable to assuage investor fears, FBG was forced to shelve the $6bn refinancing deal. In a desperate bid to restore market confidence, the company appointed Deloitte to conduct a strict "quality of earnings" review, specifically targeting its sprawling, off-balance-sheet invoice factoring programs.

The Liquidity Squeeze

Until the week of the bankruptcy filing, many lenders were unaware that FBG had billions in inventory-backed loans via off-balance-sheet Special Purpose Entities (SPEs). After filing, one lender asked how much of the $2bn advanced to FBG remained in a segregated account and was told: "$0".

Academic Research Context

Does formalizing off-balance-sheet financing help?

A Brief History of Hidden Leases

Before 2019, companies legally engaged in massive off-balance-sheet financing through Operating Leases. Airlines would lease entire fleets of planes, and retailers would lease hundreds of storefronts, but because these weren't classified as "Capital Leases," the multi-billion dollar liabilities never appeared on the balance sheet. They were hidden in the footnotes. This made companies appear far less leveraged than they actually were. Standard setters eventually fought back with ASU No. 2016-02, forcing companies to capitalize these operating leases as "Right-of-Use" assets and liabilities.

To understand the implications of FBG's hidden inventory leasing structures, we can look at academic research analyzing that exact Accounting Standards Update (ASU No. 2016-02).

1. Improved Quality

Overall financial reporting quality improves significantly after forcing off-balance-sheet items onto the books.

2. Less Asymmetry

This improvement is highly concentrated among firms that previously suffered from high information asymmetry.

3. Access to Debt

Ironically, firms exhibiting improved reporting quality used more traditional debt financing afterward, as lenders trusted their books more.

"Our evidence corroborates the argument that ASU 2016-02 increases the cost of [hidden] financing, and firms mitigate the impact by improving overall financial reporting quality."

Discussion Questions

Jot down your notes below. The instructor can unlock the model answers globally at the bottom of the page.

1 Which accounts or notes would you expect to capture factoring, supply-chain finance, and inventory-backed leasing (such as sale-leaseback or repo transactions)?

Model Answer

Factoring: If receivables are sold without recourse, they are removed from the balance sheet and disclosed under "Transfers of Financial Assets". If sold with recourse, they remain on the balance sheet as short-term borrowing.

Supply-Chain Finance (Reverse Factoring): Recent FASB updates require specific disclosures for supplier finance programs detailing terms, pending liability amounts, and balance sheet location.

Inventory-Backed Leasing / Sale-Leaseback / Repo: Under ASC 842 / IFRS 16, true leases require recognizing a Right-of-Use (ROU) asset and Lease Liability. However, if structured through unconsolidated Special Purpose Entities (SPEs), they might only be briefly mentioned in "Variable Interest Entities (VIEs)" footnotes.

2 Where should the participation of FBG executives in Onset-linked deals appear in disclosures? What controls mitigate incentive conflicts?

Model Answer

Disclosure Location: Must be disclosed in the "Related Party Transactions" footnote, detailing the relationship nature, monetary value, and specific terms (e.g., the "double-digit rates" paid by FBG).

Proposed Controls: Independent Approval by an Audit/Risk Committee, absolute Recusal Policies for executives with financial interests, and obtaining independent third-party Fairness Opinions to prove terms are at market rates.

3 Where should the boundary sit between an audit opinion on historical books and credit due diligence for cash and collateral‑intensive structures?

Model Answer

The Role of the Audit: Audits provide reasonable assurance of historical compliance with GAAP/IFRS. They verify technical rules (like SPE non-consolidation) but do not predict future liquidity crises or uncover sophisticated cash commingling hidden by paper documentation.

Credit Due Diligence: Covers forward-looking economic reality: physically tracing cash/segregated accounts, legal review of collateral priority (perfecting liens), and recognizing the true Economic Substance (a "lease" acting like high-interest debt). Auditors map the past; creditors must map future cash flow risks.

4 Would recognition rules similar to leases (ASU 2016-02) improve transparency around working-capital financing — or simply shift the activity?

Model Answer

Improving Transparency: Yes, formalizing off-balance sheet items generally improves financial reporting quality. If factoring and supply-chain finance were universally recognized as short-term debt, "headline debt" would accurately reflect leverage without rating agency adjustments.

Shifting the Activity: However, financial engineering adapts. Forcing it onto the balance sheet would likely incentivize new, more complex structures (synthetic derivatives, highly sophisticated unconsolidated SPEs) to achieve the same off-balance-sheet leverage goals.
