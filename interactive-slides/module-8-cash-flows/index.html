Why SCF Matters

The Income Statement uses Accrual Accounting (matching revenues/expenses) but ignores cash timing. This can obscure a company's true health. The Statement of Cash Flows reveals:

The 3 Activities

We classify all cash transactions into three buckets:

Operating (O)
Day-to-day business (Sales, Wages, Inventory, Interest)
Investing (I)
Long-term Assets (PP&E, Investments)
Financing (F)
Funding the business (Debt, Equity, Dividends Paid)

Rule of Thumb

Operating: Items found on the Income Statement.

Investing: Items related to Long-term Assets.

Financing: Items related to Long-term Debt & Equity.

Life Cycle Patterns

Cash flows evolve as a company matures:

  • Intro: Burning cash to grow (Neg Ops).
  • Growth: Investing heavily (Neg Inv).
  • Maturity: Cash Cow (Pos Ops, Neg Fin).
  • Decline: Liquidation (Pos Inv).

Indirect Method: The 4 Steps

The Process

  1. Start with Net Income.
  2. Adjust Non-Cash Items.
  3. Reverse Gains/Losses.
  4. Adjust Working Capital.

Formula: NI ± Adj = OCF

(Click for Example)

Why?

Net Income ($100) minus Depreciation ($20) = $80 profit, but cash didn't leave. So we start at $80 and add back $20 to get true cash of $100.

Step 2: Non-Cash Items

Expenses (e.g., Depreciation): Reduced NI, didn't use cash. ADD BACK

Revenues (e.g., Unrealized Gains): Increased NI, no cash received. SUBTRACT

ADD BACK
Unrealized Gain on Stock
SUBTRACT

Step 3: Gains/Losses

A Gain is an accounting "Paper Gain" in Operating.

We figure out the Total Investing Amount (Sale Price) and put that in Investing.

So, we remove the Gain from Operating to avoid double counting.

Gain on Sale ($500)
SUBTRACT (Ops)

Total Investing Inflow = Book Value + Gain

OCF / CapEx Ratio

Measures ability to grow without borrowing.

Microsoft (2023): $87B / $28B = 3.12

ItemAmount (B)
Operating Cash Flow$87.6
Additions to PP&E($28.1)

Interpretations

  • Ratio > 1: Conservative/Healthy. Self-funding growth.
  • Ratio < 1: May rely on external debt/equity to grow. Earnings might be artificial if OCF is consistently low.
  • Ratio dropping rapidly: Red Flag - Deteriorating efficiency or aggressive capitalization.

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

Free cash flow is defined as Operating Cash Flow minus Capital Expenditures (CapEx).

It is widely considered the "Golden Metric" in valuation because it represents the cash that a company is able to generate after laying out the money required to maintain or expand its asset base. This is the cash available for discretionary distribution to investors (both debt and equity holders) without jeopardizing the ongoing business operations.

Earnings Quality Ratio

Net Income / CFO

  • Ratio > 1: Conservative.
  • Ratio < 1: Earnings might be artificial.
  • Ratio dropping rapidly: Red Flag.

Module Summary

1. We began by establishing that cash is the lifeblood of a business, while profit is an accounting construct. The Statement of Cash Flows (SCF) bridges this gap by reconciling Net Income to Cash Flow through three key activities: Operating, Investing, and Financing. (Click next)
2. Using the Indirect Method, we learned to adjust Net Income by adding back non-cash expenses like depreciation and reversing accounting gains/losses to avoid double counting. We applied specific rules to working capital: current asset increases consume cash, while liability increases provide cash.
3. Finally, we assessed Earnings Quality. High-quality earnings are sustainable and backed by cash flow. Cases like Intel demonstrate that even profitable companies can have deteriorating quality if profits are driven by tax benefits rather than core operations.
