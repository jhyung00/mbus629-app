Beyond the Bottom Line.

Separating Signal from Noise in Asset Analysis

Objective

Investigate Long-Term Assets, Depreciation schedules, and Analyst Adjustments to uncover true economic value.

The Detective's Dilemma

Imagine you're analyzing "TechTitan Inc." They just reported a massive 50% jump in Net Income. The stock is soaring. Everyone is cheering.

But you dig deeper. You find that their actual sales of software are down.

So where did the money come from? They sold their headquarters building for a huge one-time gain.

This is the Analyst's core mission: Separating the Signal (sustainable software sales) from the Noise (one-time building sales).

Part 1: To Capitalize or Expense?

This is the first decision in accounting for any long-term asset.

Capitalize (CAPEX)

Record as an Asset on Balance Sheet.

Includes

Purchase price, transport, duties, tax, installation, testing.

Effect: Smooths cost over time via depreciation.

Expense

Record immediately on Income Statement.

Includes

Routine repairs, low-value items, most R&D.

Effect: Hits profit immediately.

Poll Question 1

You buy a trash can for the office for $20. It will last 10 years. Do you Capitalize or Expense it?

Click to Reveal Answer

EXPENSE IT!

Technically it's an asset, but due to Materiality, small items are expensed immediately to save accounting effort.

Depreciation: Using it Up

Depreciation is the allocation of cost over time. It requires three estimates:

  • Useful Life: How long will it last?
  • Salvage Value: What's it worth at the end?
  • Method: How fast does it wear out?

Straight-Line: Equal expense every year. (Most common for financial reporting)

Accelerated (MACRS): More expense early on. (Used for Tax to save cash flow)

Straight-Line Math

Let's crunch the numbers.

Machine Cost: $100,000
Salvage Value: $10,000
Life: 5 Years

(Cost - Salvage) / Life
($100,000 - $10,000) / 5
= $18,000 per year

Net Book Value (NBV)

On the Balance Sheet, the asset shrinks every year.

Year Asset Cost Accumulated Depreciation Net Book Value
Start$100,000$0$100,000
Year 1$100,000($18,000)$82,000
Year 2$100,000($36,000)$64,000
............
Year 5$100,000($90,000)$10,000 (Salvage)

Poll Question 2

Which depreciation method results in higher Net Income in Year 1: Straight-Line or Double Declining Balance?

Click to Reveal Answer

STRAIGHT-LINE

Accelerated methods (like DDB) record higher expense in early years, which lowers income. Straight-line has lower expense early on, leading to higher income.

The R&D Debate

Research & Development creates future value. It feels like an asset. But GAAP is conservative.

Personnel & Ideas

EXPENSE IMMEDIATELY

Future benefits are too uncertain.

Project-Specific Machines

EXPENSE IMMEDIATELY

If it has no alternative use, it's a sunk cost.

General Labs & Buildings

CAPITALIZE

Because they can be used for other things later.

The Deeper Insight

Here is a secret:

The Gain is just a correction of history.

If you have a Gain: It means you depreciated the asset too much in the past. You told shareholders profits were lower than they really were.

If you have a Loss: It means you didn't depreciate enough. You overstated past profits.

Poll Question 3

We sell a truck for $5,000. Its Net Book Value is $8,000. We record a $3,000 Loss. Did we depreciate too much or too little in the past?

Click to Reveal Answer

TOO LITTLE

We thought the truck was worth $8,000 on our books, but the market said it was only worth $5,000. We didn't expense (depreciate) enough value in prior years.

Case Study: T-Mobile Wireline Sale

In 2022, T-Mobile sold wireline assets and recorded a $1 Billion Gain.

As an analyst, you have two jobs:

  1. Reversal: Remove the $1B gain from 2022 (it's not recurring).
  2. Allocation: Spread that $1B back over the years the asset was used (correcting the past).

Assumption: Assets were used for 10 years.

Real Life: CVS Health (2021)

CVS decided to close 900 stores. The assets in those stores (shelves, signs, lease rights) were suddenly worth much less.

The Hit: $1,358 Million Impairment Charge.

The Reality: This is a non-cash expense. The cash was spent years ago to build the stores. This charge just acknowledges the mistake in valuation.

Restructuring: The "Big Bath"

Companies often group bad news into one big "Restructuring" charge to clear the decks.

Components:

  • Employee Severance: Cash to be paid to fired workers.
  • Asset Write-downs: Non-cash reduction of asset values.
  • Exit Costs: Fees to break contracts/leases.

Part 3: Diagnosing PP&E Health

Is the factory productive? Are the machines falling apart? Check these vitals:

PP&E Turnover

Measures revenue generated per dollar of fixed assets.

Average Useful Life

Depreciable Cost / Depreciation Expense
How long do assets last?

Percent Used Up

Accum. Depr / Depreciable Cost
Are we due for a massive replacement cycle?

PP&E Turnover: Interpreting the Ratio

HIGH Turnover

Efficient

Generating lots of sales with few assets (or assets are old/fully depreciated).

LOW Turnover

Capital Intensive

Heavy investment required to generate sales (e.g., Utilities, Oil, Telecom).

Home Depot Turnover (2022)

$$ \text{PP&E Turnover} = \frac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Average Net PP&E}} $$

TAP TO REVEAL

$157,403 / $25,415 = 6.2 times

Home Depot generates $6.20 in Sales per $1 of Net PP&E.

Average Useful Life

$$ \text{Avg Useful Life} = \frac{\text{Gross Depreciable Assets}}{\text{Depreciation Expense}} $$

How long does Home Depot keep its stuff?

TAP TO REVEAL

$41,916 / $2,756 = 15.2 Years

This suggests their equipment and buildings last about 15 years on average.

Percent Used Up

$$ \text{Percent Used Up} = \frac{\text{Accumulated Depreciation}}{\text{Gross Depreciable Cost}} $$

How worn out is the fleet?

TAP TO REVEAL

$26,644 / $42,259 = 63%

Interpretation: Home Depot's assets are 63% "gone". They will likely need heavy CAPEX soon to replace them.

Part 4: The Unseen Value

For many companies, physical assets are irrelevant. The value is in Brands, Patents, and Code.

The Gap: Market Value is often 3x-4x higher than Book Value. Why? Because internally developed intangibles (like the Nike Swoosh) are usually not on the balance sheet.

They only appear when bought.

The Birth of Goodwill

How is Goodwill created? Let's look at AMD acquiring Xilinx (2022).

Purchase Price$48,793 M
Less: Fair Value of Net Assets($26,009 M)
Equals: Goodwill $22,784 M

Goodwill is the "Plug." It's the premium paid over the value of identifiable stuff.

Warren Buffett on Goodwill

Key Takeaways

  • Business Attractiveness: Ignore goodwill. Look at Return on Tangible Assets.
  • Capital Allocation: You MUST include goodwill to judge management, because they spent real money to buy it.
  • Amortization vs Write-offs: Amortization makes no sense (brands often gain value). But write-offs are appropriate when a purchase was a mistake.
  • The Lubrizol Example: Good business on its own (high return on tangible assets), but Berkshire's return is lower because of the premium paid.

Goodwill: The "Restaurant Analogy"

What are you paying for?

If you buy a popular restaurant, you pay for the ovens and tables (Identifiable Assets). But you pay EXTRA for the reputation and loyal customers.

That EXTRA is Goodwill.

How do they value the parts?

AMD identified $27B in specific intangibles. How?

Relief from Royalty

"If we didn't own this brand, how much would we have to pay to license it?" (Used for Brands).

Replacement Cost

"How much would it cost to rebuild this code from scratch?" (Used for Software).

Excess Earnings

"How much cash does this generate above a normal return on assets?"

When Goodwill Goes Bad

Goodwill is not amortized. It sits there forever... unless it is Impaired.

Impairment Signal

A Goodwill Write-down means management admits: "We overpaid."

It's an admission of a failed acquisition strategy.

Case Study: DXC Technology

In 2020, DXC took a massive $6.8 Billion impairment charge on goodwill from a deal 4 years prior.

As an analyst, seeing this huge loss in 2020 is "Noise." It distorts 2020 performance.

The loss really belongs to the years 2017, 2018, 2019, and 2020 (when value was eroding).

DXC Adjustment: Spreading the Pain

We take the $6.8B loss and spread it back over 4 years ($1.7B/year).

Metric ($B)2017201820192020
Allocation of Loss($1.7)($1.7)($1.7)($1.7)
Tax Benefit$0.4$0.4$0.4$0.4
Net Income Impact($1.3)($1.3)($1.3)($1.3)

Result: 2020 Income increases (removing the huge hit), but prior years' income decreases.

Case Study: Meta's $90 Billion Bet

Less than five years after Mark Zuckerberg laid out his vision for the metaverse, that idea is officially dead. Meta has begun laying off people in Reality Labs to refocus on AI-powered smartglasses.

Zuckerberg compares the shift to AI glasses (partnered with Ray-Ban/Oakley) to the rise of smartphones. "It's hard to imagine a world in several years where most glasses that people wear aren't AI glasses," he said.
